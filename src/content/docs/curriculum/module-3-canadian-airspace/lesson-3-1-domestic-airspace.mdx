# Lesson 3.1: The Structure of Canadian Airspace

To navigate legally and safely, you need to understand how Canada divides its sky geographically, vertically, and how airspace is structured around airports and navigation aids.

## 1. Geographic and Vertical Divisions

Canadian Domestic Airspace is divided in two distinct ways:

#### Geographic Divisions (North vs. South)

- **Southern Domestic Airspace (SDA):** Magnetic compass indications are reliable. Cruising altitudes and tracks are based on **Magnetic North**.
- **Northern Domestic Airspace (NDA):** Magnetic compasses can be erratic near the magnetic pole. Cruising altitudes and tracks are based on **True North**.

#### Vertical Divisions (High vs. Low)

- **Low Level Airspace:** All airspace from the surface up to, but not including, **18,000 feet ASL**.
- **High Level Airspace:** All airspace from **18,000 feet ASL and above**.

## 2. The "Inverted Cake" Model

Airspace around major certified aerodromes (airports) is shaped like an upside-down wedding cake. This protects aircraft as they climb and descend, while allowing VFR traffic to safely fly underneath the outer tiers.

> [Placeholder: Image - "Inverted Cake" diagram from page 21 of the Study Guide showing a CZ at the bottom, surrounded by higher TCA/CAE layers]

- **Control Zone (CZ):** The bottom layer centered on the airport. It always starts at ground level and extends to a specified altitude ASL. Not all airports have them, and some are only active during certain hours.
- **Terminal Control Area (TCA) & Control Area Extension (CAE):** These are the wider, upper layers surrounding the CZ.
  - TCAs contain **Class C or D** airspace.
  - CAEs contain **Class E** airspace.
  - They have specific "floors" (e.g., 700', 1,500', 2,500', or 4,000' ASL) allowing you to fly uncontrolled beneath them.

## 3. Low-Level Airways

Low-level airways are designated flight paths between two radio navigation aids (VORs and/or NDBs). While modern GPS has changed how we navigate, you must know these specific dimensions for the exam.

### Vertical Dimensions & Airspace Class

- **Floor to Ceiling:** Low-level airways extend upward from **2,200' AGL** up to, but not including, **18,000' ASL**.
- **Airspace Class:** The airway is **Class E** from **2,200' AGL** up to **12,500' ASL**. It becomes **Class B** from **12,500' ASL** up to **18,000' ASL**.

### Horizontal Dimensions (Width & Expansion)

Because radio navigation signals spread out the further you get from the beacon, the width of the airway expands (or "splays") on longer routes.

#### VHF/UHF Airways (Based on two VORs)

- **Basic Width:** **4 NM** on each side of the centreline (**8 NM total width**).
- **Expansion:** The width increases between points where lines diverging at **4.5°** on each side of the centreline intersect the basic width boundary.

#### LF/MF Airways (Based on two NDBs)

- **Basic Width:** **4.34 NM** on each side of the centreline (**8.68 NM total width**).
- **Expansion:** The width increases between points where lines diverging at **5°** on each side of the centreline intersect the basic width boundary.

#### Mixed Airways (Based on a VOR and an NDB)

- These share the same dimensions as the LF/MF airway (**4.34 NM** basic width, expanding at **5°**).
