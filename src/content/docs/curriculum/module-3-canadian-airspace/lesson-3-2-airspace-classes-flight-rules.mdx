# Lesson 3.2: Airspace Classes (A to G) & Flight Rules

All Canadian airspace is designated by a letter from A to G. Class G is uncontrolled, Class F is special use, and Classes A through E are controlled.

## 1. Uncontrolled Airspace (Class G)

- **Definition:** Any airspace not specifically designated as controlled. If it is not specifically identified as controlled airspace on a VNC chart, it is Class G.
- **Limits:** Typically exists from the ground up to 18,000' ASL (unless capped by a layer of controlled airspace above it).
  - **Exam Note:** In the Northern Domestic Airspace, Class G also exists between 18,000' ASL and Flight Level 230.
- **Rules:** You do not need the HAGAR to fly in Class G. However, all non-airspace-specific CARs still apply.

## 2. Controlled Airspace (Classes A, B, C, D)

Controlled airspace is where Air Traffic Control (ATC) service is provided. You must know the entry requirements for each class.

| Airspace Class | Where is it?                                    | Rules for Hang Gliders / VFR Pilots                                                                                  |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Class A**    | High Level (18,000' ASL+ in SDA, FL230+ in NDA) | IFR only. Hang gliders are not allowed.                                                                              |
| **Class B**    | 12,500' ASL up to 18,000' ASL                   | Both IFR and VFR allowed. Requires ATC clearance. Impractical for hang gliders without special pre-arrangement.      |
| **Class C**    | TCAs and CZs around major airports              | Requires a 2-way radio and ATC clearance to enter. ATC can deny you entry.                                           |
| **Class D**    | TCAs and CZs around busy airports               | Requires a 2-way radio and establishing two-way communication. ATC cannot deny you entry if you meet the conditions. |

:::note[Exam crossover from Air Regulations]

- **Aerobatic flight limitation:** No aircraft may conduct aerobatic flight over any urban or other populous area.
  :::

## 3. Class E Airspace & The Hang Glider Exemption

Class E airspace is designated where there is an operational need for control, but it doesn't require the strict rules of Classes A through D. It is found in CAEs, low-level airways, and CZs without operating control towers.

Highly Testable: To legally operate a hang glider or paraglider in Class E airspace, you must meet all of the following conditions:

- The pilot is at least **16 years of age**.
- The pilot holds a **Category 1, 3, or 4 Medical Certificate**.
- The pilot has passed the **HAGAR examination (minimum 60%)**.
- The glider is equipped with a **magnetic compass** and an **altimeter**.
- The flight is a **cross-country flight**.
- The pilot informs the nearest **Flight Service Station (FSS)** of the time of departure and estimated duration of the flight in Class E airspace.

## 4. Special Use Airspace (Class F)

Class F airspace is established to confine specific activities or hazards. It can be controlled, uncontrolled, or a combination of both. It is broken into two main categories:

- **CYA (Advisory):** Activities like parachuting or flight training happen here. You can enter an active CYA (if you are legally allowed in the underlying airspace), but you must exercise extreme caution.
- **CYR (Restricted):** You cannot enter an active CYR unless authorization has been obtained from the user agency.

### Class F Activity Codes (Memorize these)

When you look at a VNC chart, the letter in the brackets identifies the hazard (e.g., CYA 516**(P)**).

- **A** - Aerobatics
- **F** - Aircraft test area
- **H** - Hang gliding
- **M** - Military operations
- **P** - Parachuting
- **S** - Soaring
- **T** - Training

Below is one example of a CYA, in this case CYA 516(P) in Southern Ontario. As you can see, the VNC has all of the conditions associated with the CYA. In this case, the CYA is active for parachuting operations during daylight hours every Friday, Saturday, Sunday and Holidays between 1 Apr and 31 Oct from the ground up to 12,500 ASL.

On occasion, the CYA will be active on other days and/or will extend up to 14,000 ASL and that will be promulgated by NOTAM.

![Example of CYA 516(P) on a VNC chart](/images/cyp-example.avif)

Note that there is an aerodrome in the middle of the CYA which is consistent with parachuting operations. The plane has to take off from somewhere and it is efficient to have the parachutists land where the plane takes off from.
