# Lesson 1.1: Definitions & Exemptions

Before looking at the operational rules, you must understand the legal language used by Transport Canada and how your glider fits into the Canadian Aviation Regulations (CARs).

## 1. Key Definitions

- **Aircraft:** Any man-made object that flies. A hang glider/paraglider is considered a **glider**, which is an aircraft.
- **Aerodrome vs. Airport:**
  - **Aerodrome:** Any facility used for the take-off and landing of aircraft.
  - **Airport:** A specifically certified aerodrome that meets strict safety requirements.
- **Day / Daylight:** The time between the beginning of **morning civil twilight** and the end of **evening civil twilight**.
  - Civil twilight is when the center of the sun's disk is **6 degrees below the horizon**.
- **Night:** The time between the end of evening civil twilight and the beginning of morning civil twilight.
- **Flight Level (FL):** Altitude expressed in hundreds of feet with an altimeter set to standard pressure (**29.92 inHg**). For example, **FL 240** is **24,000 ft**.

## 2. The Hang Glider Exemptions (Section 1.3)

Transport Canada grants hang gliders specific exemptions from standard aircraft regulations.

You are **exempt** from:

- Requiring pilot licenses or permits.
- Aircraft registration and displaying nationality marks.
- Airworthiness certification (no Certificate of Airworthiness or flight permit required).
- The requirement to maintain and carry log books.
- Carrying day VFR instruments (except a compass/altimeter in specific airspaces).
- Safety belt and harness requirements.
- The requirement to carry an Emergency Locator Transmitter (ELT).

:::note[HPAC/ACVL and Transport Canada]
Many aspects of hang gliding in Canada are mostly self-regulated. HPAC/ACVL has put in place its own regulations as appropriate. An example is the pilot certification program.

It is important to understand that Transport Canada (TC) has not delegated its responsibilities to regulate hang gliding to HPAC/ACVL. HPAC/ACVL regulations apply only to its members and nobody else.

TC's decision not to over-regulate hang gliding is based on the existence of HPAC/ACVL and its assumption that HPAC/ACVL makes an effort to educate Canadian HG/PG pilots to share Canadian airspace safely with other aircraft. If TC loses trust in HPAC/ACVL's ability to do so, it could impose additional rules.
:::
